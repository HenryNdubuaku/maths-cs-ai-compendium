# Basis and Duality: The Math of Translating Maps

Imagine you and your friend are meeting at a café. You both have maps of the city, but your maps are drawn completely differently. This repository explains the math of how you translate between your map and your friend's map, covering concepts behind PCA, feature transforms, and attention queries in Machine Learning.

## 1. The Building Blocks: Basis

A **basis** is the set of basic directions you use to get around. Think of them as your building blocks. 

*   **Your Map (The Standard Basis):** You navigate using a normal grid of city streets. Your two main directions are walking East (the x-axis) and walking North (the y-axis). In math, East is `(1, 0)` and North is `(0, 1)`.

When you say the café is at coordinates **`(3, 2)`**, you are giving a recipe: *"Walk 3 blocks East, then 2 blocks North."* Those numbers (3 and 2) are your **coordinates**. 

---

## 2. Changing Languages: Change of Basis

Your friend's map doesn't use North and East. Their side of the city is built on diagonal streets!

*   Their first building block goes North-East: `(1, 1)`.
*   Their second building block goes North-West: `(-1, 1)`.

A **change of basis** is a mathematical dictionary that translates your directions into their directions. 
*   **Your map says:** `(3, 2)`.
*   **Their map says:** `(2.5, -0.5)`. This means *"walk 2.5 diagonal blocks one way, and half a block backwards the other way."*

The café never moved! You are just describing how to get there using two different languages.

---

## 3. The Rulers: Duality

When you walk to the café, imagine someone is standing on the East street with a tape measure, and someone else is standing on the North street. 

*   The East ruler *only* cares about how far East you went. It shouts: "You went 3 blocks!"
*   The North ruler *only* cares about North. It shouts: "You went 2 blocks!"

In math, these "rulers" are called **Linear Functionals**, and the complete set of rulers for your map is called the **Dual Space**. 

Every map comes with its own perfectly matched set of rulers. Your friend's diagonal map has diagonal rulers designed to specifically measure how far you walked along *their* paths. 

**Coordinates are just the readings on these rulers.** When you translate from your map to your friend's map, you are swapping out your standard rulers for their diagonal rulers to get the new numbers.

---

## 4. The Python Code (JAX)

Here is the math in action using `jax.numpy`.

### Task 1: Translating the Café's Coordinates

```python
import jax.numpy as jnp

# Your street map coordinates for the café
v = jnp.array([3.0, 2.0])
print(f"Standard basis coords: {v}")

# Your friend's diagonal map paths (the new basis)
P = jnp.array([[1.0, -1.0],
               [1.0,  1.0]])

# Translate the coordinates
new_coords = jnp.linalg.solve(P, v)
print(f"New basis coords: {new_coords}")
```
*Output: `[ 2.5 -0.5]`*

### Task 2: Building the Rulers (Dual Basis)

We find the custom rulers by taking the inverse of the matrix (`P⁻¹`).

```python
# Create the rulers by flipping the dictionary upside down
P_inv = jnp.linalg.inv(P)
b1_star, b2_star = P_inv[0], P_inv[1]
print(f"Ruler 1 (b1*): {b1_star}, Ruler 2 (b2*): {b2_star}")

# Measure your street coordinates with the new rulers
print(f"Coordinate 1 = {jnp.dot(b1_star, v)}")  # Outputs: 2.5
print(f"Coordinate 2 = {jnp.dot(b2_star, v)}")  # Outputs: -0.5

# THE TRAP: Using a path as its own ruler gives the wrong answer!
print(f"Wrong measurement = {jnp.dot(P[:, 0], v)}") # Outputs: 5.0
```

### What is `P[:, 0]`?
`P[:, 0]` is Python's shorthand for *"Look at the grid `P`, and give me everything in the very first column."* 
Because our dictionary `P` is stacked as:
```python
P = [[1.0, -1.0], 
     [1.0,  1.0]]
```
Slicing down the first column gives `[1.0, 1.0]` (your friend's North-East walking path). The final line proves that dotting a walking path with the café's location (`5.0`) gives the wrong coordinate. You **must** use the custom dual ruler (`2.5`)!
